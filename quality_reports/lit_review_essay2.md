## Summary

Three literatures converge on this paper. (1) **Influencer marketing** research established that creators drive engagement through content, credibility, and expertise, but has "largely focused on content that mentions brands" and treated engagement as a single quantity. (2) **Sponsorship-disclosure** research is genuinely *mixed*: disclosure reliably raises advertising recognition and can trigger persuasion knowledge and less favorable responses, yet a growing set of studies finds disclosure *increases* engagement via transparency/trust. (3) **Engagement-typology** work distinguishes amplification (shares/retweets, public word-of-mouth) from conversation (replies, more private), and shows the two are driven by different mechanisms — the crux of this paper's contribution. The **Persuasion Knowledge Model** (Friestad & Wright 1994) supplies the mechanism, and **expectancy (dis)confirmation / violation** supplies the "surprise" logic.

## Theme 1 — Influencer marketing: effectiveness and market context

## Theme 2 — Sponsorship disclosure: the mixed-effects debate

**Disclosure as a cost (recognition → resistance):**
- **Boerman, Willemsen & Van Der Aa (2017, *JIM*)** — "This post is sponsored": disclosure activates persuasion knowledge and lowers eWOM. The clearest disclosure→PK→engagement chain.
- **Campbell, Mohr & Verlegh (2013, *JCP*)** — disclosures can help consumers *resist* covert persuasion (timing/type matter).
- **Eisend et al. (2020, *JA*)** — **meta-analysis** of disclosing sponsored content; the authoritative "what disclosure does on average" cite.
- **Evans et al. (2017, *JIadv*)** — Instagram disclosure language raises ad recognition, lowers behavioral intent.
- Also: Colliander & Erlandsson (2015), Avramova/Dens/De Pelsmacker (2022), Boerman/Van Reijmersdal/Neijens (2014, timing), Giuffredi-Kähr et al. (2022), De Jans & Hudders (2020), Cheng & Zhang (2025, *Mgmt Sci*, "reputation burning").

**Disclosure as a benefit (transparency → trust):**
- **Cao & Belo (2023, *MISQ*)** — explicit disclosure *increases* engagement; transparency mechanism. The strongest "disclosure helps" cite and closest empirical sibling.
- **Evans, Wojdynski & Grubbs Hoy (2019, *IJA*)** — transparency mitigates the negative recognition effect.
- Abendroth & Heyman (2013); Hwang & Jeong (2016); Wang & Wang (2019); Wang, Xu & Wang (2018); Zhang, Lee & Tan (2023, YouTube/FTC).

**Measurement anchor (critical):**
- **Ershov, He & Seiler (2025, *Marketing Science*)** — "How Much Influencer Marketing Is Undisclosed? Evidence from Twitter." Provides the disclosed/undisclosed/organic classification approach your data build uses, and the "~96% of sponsored content is undisclosed" fact. This is the methodological backbone of your treatment variable — cite prominently in §2.

## Theme 3 — Persuasion Knowledge Model (mechanism)

- **Friestad & Wright (1994, *JCR*)** — the PKM: consumers deploy persuasion knowledge to cope with persuasion attempts; recognizing an attempt changes the response. The theoretical engine for your promotional-intent mechanism.
- **Wojdynski & Evans (2020, *IJA*)** — the **CARE model** (Covert Advertising Recognition and Effects): PKM specialized to native/masked formats. Directly on point for *undisclosed* (covert) posts and a strong addition to your mechanism section.
- **Grigsby & Mellema (2020, *JIM*)** — PK activation from storytelling in native advertising; supports "multiple covert-intent signals amplify PK."
- **Main, Dahl & Darke (2007, *JCP*)** and **Fein (1996, *JPSP*)** — suspicion / sinister-attribution; the "surprise triggers suspicion of covert motive" step.

## Theme 4 — Expectancy (dis)confirmation and violation ("surprise")

- **Oliver (1980, *JMR*)** and **Churchill & Surprenant (1982, *JMR*)** — expectancy-*disconfirmation*: deviation from expected performance drives (dis)satisfaction. This is what your draft currently uses for the "deviation from usual content" argument, via **Pansari & Kumar (2017)** for the engagement link.
- **Candidate addition (verify before citing):** *Expectancy Violations Theory* (Burgoon tradition) is the natural home for a **"disclosure surprise"** construct — it models reactions to *unexpected* behavior directly, whereas expectancy-disconfirmation is about satisfaction. Web search confirms EVT is actively applied in marketing/advertising, but I did not verify a specific citation's details — **source a canonical EVT cite (e.g., Burgoon) yourself before adding.** Do not ship an EVT cite I haven't grounded.

## Theme 5 — Engagement typology: amplification (retweets) vs conversation (replies)

This is your contribution's fulcrum and your draft already frames it well:
- **Zhang, Moe & Schweidel (2017, *IJRM*)** — modeling rebroadcasting (shares); shares as public word-of-mouth. Core cite for "retweet = amplification."
- **Hoffman & Fodor (2010, *MIT SMR*)** — shares as WOM/ROI.
- **Gong et al. (2017, *JMR*)** and **Lambrecht, Tucker & Wiertz (2018, *Marketing Science*)** — Twitter-marketing field experiments; note both focus on *shares*, motivating your "we also study replies" extension.
- **Choi et al. (2018, *JIM*)** — disclosure timing/prominence → intention to *share* branded content; directly links disclosure to the sharing (amplification) mode.
- **Virality drivers (positive emotion):** Berger & Milkman (2012, *JMR*); Tellis et al. (2019, *JM*); Heimbach & Hinz (2016, *IJRM*); Han, Lappas & Sabnis (2020, *ISR*); Berger, Kim & Meyer (2021, *JCR*); Vosoughi, Roy & Aral (2018, *Science*).

Real journals, but I have not verified volume/pages — confirm before adding.

## Gaps and opportunities (positioning)

1. **G1 — Engagement is treated as one thing.** Twitter-marketing work centers *shares*; disclosure work centers attitudes/intentions. Few papers estimate disclosure/content effects **separately for amplification (retweets) and conversation (replies)** and show them *diverging*. Your retweet-up / reply-down result is exactly this gap.
2. **G2 — Disclosure literature is about *presence*, not *expectedness*.** The mixed disclosure findings may partly reflect an omitted moderator: how *surprising* the disclosure/brand behavior is relative to the creator's baseline. "Disclosure surprise" reframes the mixed evidence.
3. **G3 — Theory gap for "surprise."** Your current apparatus (expectancy-disconfirmation + PKM) explains *why disclosure changes processing* but not cleanly *why surprise per se* moves amplification and conversation in opposite directions. Expectancy-Violation Theory + the amplification/conversation distinction is the missing bridge — and the reason G3 must be closed before the Intro citations are finalized.
4. **G4 — Observational scale vs. experimental disclosure lit.** Most disclosure evidence is lab-experimental (Boerman, Evans, Campbell). Your 281,837-post observational design with influencer/time FE and clustered inference complements that with field external validity — a contribution to state explicitly.
